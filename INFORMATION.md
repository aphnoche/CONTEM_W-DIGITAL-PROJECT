## Main Topic: Human Rights Violations

### Subtopic 1: EJK (War on Drugs) — Philippines, Duterte Administration

#### What is EJK?

Extrajudicial killing occurs when someone in an official position deliberately kills a person without any legal process. In Duterte's war on drugs, the state and police were the primary actors.

#### Key Facts

- **Period:** 2016–2022 (Duterte's presidential term)
- **Official death toll:** ~6,000
- **Estimated by human rights organizations:** 12,000–30,000+ (including civilians)

#### Case Studies

| Victim | Date | Summary |
|---|---|---|
| **Florjohn Cruz** | October 2016 | Shot inside his home while fixing a radio. Police entered without a buy-bust operation. His mother heard him plead for his life. Police and family accounts conflict. ([Source](https://www.amnesty.org/en/projects/the-philippines-war-on-drugs/)) |
| **Kian Delos Santos** | August 2017 | 17-year-old gunned down in an alleyway. Police claimed self-defense; CCTV showed him unarmed and dragged. Only 1 of 4 officers convicted. Sparked national outrage. |
| **Kim Lester Ramos** | October 2019 | Shot point-blank while helping an injured friend. Police claimed he tried to snatch a gun; witnesses say he was unarmed. ([Source](https://www.rappler.com/features/newsbreak/in-depth/242358-new-case-extrajudicial-killing-marikina/)) |

#### Countries Affected

| Country | How Affected |
|---|---|
| **Iceland** | Led the 2019 UNHRC resolution demanding an investigation into Philippine EJK — 18 nations voted in favor |
| **Argentina, Australia, Austria, Denmark, Italy, Mexico, Peru, Spain, UK, Ukraine, Uruguay** | Voted in favor of the UNHRC resolution condemning the killings |
| **China, Cuba, Egypt, India, Saudi Arabia** | Opposed the UNHRC resolution, creating diplomatic divisions |

#### Impact on Globalization

- Widely criticized by international human rights groups.
- The ICC launched investigations into possible crimes against humanity.
- Created political divisions — some governments condemned the killings, others supported the anti-drug campaign.
- Weakened trust in justice systems and democratic institutions through fear and witness intimidation.
- Shifted global drug policy discourse toward health-based and human-rights-focused approaches. Key organizations: UN, ICC, Global Commission on Drug Policy, World Organization Against Torture, TRIAL International.

#### Current Status

- No comprehensive solution implemented for accountability.
- Killings reduced under the Marcos Jr. administration, which shifted focus to rehabilitation and reintegration.
- No accountability for those responsible in government and law enforcement.

### Subtopic 2: Human Rights Violations in Gaza and the Wider Middle East Conflict

#### Key Facts

- **Death toll:** Over 69,000 Palestinians killed (including 19,000 children). Findings suggest Israel committed genocide in Gaza.
- **October 7 attack:** Hamas militants killed Israeli civilians and held ~100 hostages (many subjected to torture or sexual violence). Gaza's civilian death toll remains far larger.
- **Blockade (early 2025):** 11-week total blockade — no food, fuel, medicine, or aid allowed in. 463 Palestinians (157 children) died of starvation/malnutrition.
- **Displacement:** Over 1.2 million Gazans (~70% of population) forcibly displaced by October 2025.
- **Infrastructure destruction:** All 36 hospitals damaged or destroyed. 81% of buildings destroyed. Water, electrical systems cut off.
- **Detention & torture:** Thousands of Palestinians experienced arbitrary detention and torture, violating international humanitarian law.
- **Legal action:** ICJ ordered Israel to prevent genocidal acts and allow aid. ICC pursued arrest warrants for war crimes. Compliance and enforcement remain limited.

#### Countries Affected

| Country | How Affected |
|---|---|
| **Egypt** | Suez Canal revenue collapsed due to Red Sea shipping disruption; faces pressure over refugee intake |
| **Jordan** | Faces political pressure over Palestinian refugee intake |
| **Lebanon** | Direct conflict escalation with Israel — deaths, displacement, and infrastructure damage |
| **Turkey** | Recalled ambassador from Israel; suspended trade over Gaza offensive |
| **South Africa** | Filed ICJ genocide case against Israel over Gaza |
| **Yemen** | Houthi Red Sea shipping attacks in solidarity with Gaza disrupted global trade routes |
| **Germany** | 2nd largest arms supplier to Israel (~33% of imports); suspended Gaza-bound arms exports Aug 2025 |
| **United Kingdom** | Arms export pressure; partner in F-35 program supplying Israel |
| **Colombia** | Severed diplomatic relations with Israel over Gaza; also refused US deportation flights |
| **Bolivia, Belize, Nicaragua** | Also severed diplomatic relations with Israel |
| **Bahrain, Chad, Chile, Honduras, South Africa, Turkey** | Recalled ambassadors from Israel |

#### Impact on Globalization

- Massive humanitarian funding required, straining the global humanitarian system.
- Diplomatic polarization between Global North and Global South. Weakened trust in international law enforcement.
- Regional instability increased investor uncertainty, affected markets, and contributed to oil price volatility.
- Egypt and Jordan face political pressure over refugee intake.
- Red Sea shipping disruptions, rerouted vessels, and airline cancellations slowed global supply chains, raised freight/energy costs, and harmed regional tourism.
- Western governments faced legal/diplomatic pressure over arms exports to Israel. International attention diverted from Ukraine and Sudan crises.

#### Solutions

- **Short-term (existing):** Temporary ceasefires only. No comprehensive peace agreement, no reconstruction framework.
- **UN action:** General Assembly passed ceasefire resolutions (non-binding). UNHRC launched investigations, warned about starvation as genocide and ethnic cleansing.
- **G7 & European states:** Advocate de-escalation, diplomacy, tighter sanctions, limits on offensive arms transfers, increased humanitarian access.
- **Long-term proposals:** Regional mediation, renewed ceasefires, two-state solution, international security guarantees.

#### Current Status (as of March 2026)

- Crisis remains severe. Many hospitals nonfunctional. Basic necessities (food, water, shelter, electricity) unavailable to civilians.
- Displacement ongoing. Cross-border strikes, proxy conflicts, and missile attacks continue.
- Reconstruction slow due to insecurity and aid restrictions.

### Subtopic 3: ICE (War on Immigrants) — U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement & Mass Detention

#### Key Facts

- **Detention levels (Feb 2026):** 68,289 individuals in physical custody — record high.
- **Profile of detained:** 73.6% have no criminal convictions. Many held only for minor offenses like traffic violations.
- **Deaths in custody (2025):** Tripled to 32 fatalities compared to the prior year. Thousands housed in overcrowded tent camps.
- **Racial profiling:** Race and ethnicity used as factors for reasonable suspicion, predominantly affecting Latino, Black, and Indigenous communities.
- **Budget:** Congress authorized $45 billion through 2029 for detention. No-bid contracts flow to private prison corporations (CoreCivic, GEO Group).
- **287(g) program:** Deputizes local law enforcement as federal immigration agents. Now covers jurisdictions with 32% of the U.S. population. Causes severe psychological trauma, especially among children separated from detained parents.

#### Countries Affected

| Country | How Affected |
|---|---|
| **Mexico** | Primary deportation destination; Mexican detainees increased nearly 8x between 2024–2025 |
| **Guatemala** | ~25% of all US deportation flights in 2025; used as third-country transit point |
| **Honduras** | ~20% of deportation flights; used as stopover for Venezuelan deportees |
| **El Salvador** | Received 200+ deported Venezuelans into maximum security prisons |
| **Haiti** | TPS stripped from nationals; families blocked from asylum access |
| **Venezuela** | TPS rescinded for ~350,000 nationals; deportation flights routed via Honduras |
| **Ecuador** | Highest deportation flights in South America; twice-weekly flights in 2025 |
| **Nicaragua** | Record 10 deportation flights in Sept 2025; legal status stripped from nationals |
| **Colombia** | President Petro briefly revoked authorization for US deportation flights (Jan 2025) |
| **Cuba, Peru, Dominican Republic, Jamaica** | Also received deportation flights throughout 2025 |

#### Impact on Globalization

- Mass deportation flights strained diplomatic relations with Latin American nations, triggering trade retaliations and security tensions.
- Colombia briefly closed its airspace to US military deportation flights in January 2025, prompting tariff threats.
- Chilling effect on global immigration — countries worldwide observed and mirrored restrictive enforcement policies.
- Economic disruption to US industries dependent on immigrant labor (agriculture, construction, services).
- International criticism from human rights organizations (ACLU, Human Rights First, Amnesty International) drew comparisons to authoritarian deportation practices.
- Remittance flows disrupted — billions in annual remittances to Latin American economies threatened by mass detentions.

#### Solutions

- **Current/feasible:** Community groups urging Congress to block the 2026 Homeland Security budget, end private prison contracts, and close the 287(g) program.
- **Long-term proposals:** Experts recommend updating the immigration system — expanding work, family, and refugee visas to clear waitlists; setting up legal asylum application centers before the border.

#### Current Status (as of March 2026)

- Highly active and at inhumane levels. 68,000+ in physical custody; 179,991 monitored via electronic Alternatives to Detention (ATD).
- February 2026 Fifth Circuit ruling allows indefinite detention of certain undocumented immigrants without bond hearings, stripping judicial review rights.
- Family separations, mass detentions, and interior enforcement operations remain ongoing nationwide.

## Advocacy Statement

Across all subtopics, war and state-sanctioned violence are core drivers of human rights violations. A clear pattern of power inequality emerges — those in positions of authority declare and execute violence, while civilians bear the consequences. Citizens lose their rights to live peacefully and have basic necessities met. **We stand against wars and all acts of human rights violation, including discrimination, inequality, and oppression.**
